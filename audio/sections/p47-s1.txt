Let’s dive into the relationship between NL and PSPACE. First, we need to understand that NL, or nondeterministic logarithmic space, is a complexity class that deals with problems solvable by a nondeterministic machine using logarithmic space. On the other hand, PSPACE encompasses problems that can be solved using polynomial space. 

So, how do we prove that NL is strictly contained in PSPACE? We start with Savitch's theorem, which tells us that if you have a nondeterministic algorithm operating in logarithmic space, you can simulate it using a deterministic algorithm that requires at most quadratic logarithmic space. In simpler terms, it shows that NL can fit within SPACE(log squared n), which is still a subset of polynomial space.

Next, we bring in the Space Hierarchy Theorem. This theorem states that for any space-constructible function, if you take a function f and multiply it by an unbounded function g, the space of f is strictly contained in the space of f times g. So, applying this, we see that SPACE(log squared n) is strictly contained in SPACE(log squared n times log log n) and so forth, eventually leading up to PSPACE.

Putting it all together, we find that NL is not just inside PSPACE, but it’s strictly contained within it. While we understand that there’s a gap between NL and PSPACE, the exact relationship within that gap remains open for exploration. It's even conjectured that NL might be strictly less than P, and P might be strictly less than PSPACE, but we just don’t have enough evidence to confirm that yet. So, there’s still a lot of fascinating ground to cover in this area!
