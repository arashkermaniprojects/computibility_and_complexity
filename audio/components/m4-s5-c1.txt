The Cobham–Edmonds thesis is the bet the entire field makes. It says that polynomial time is the right mathematical proxy for feasibly computable in practice. There are three reasons to believe it. First, polynomial time is robust — single-tape, multi-tape, random-access, all give you the same class P. Second, it is closed under composition — a polynomial in a polynomial is still a polynomial, so building algorithms out of polynomial-time subroutines keeps you inside P. Third, in practice almost every problem that turns out to be in P has an algorithm that is actually usable. This is a thesis, not a theorem, but six decades of empirical evidence have not seriously dented it.
