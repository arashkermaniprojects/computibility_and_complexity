Let's talk about the relationship between time and space in the context of Turing machines, often referred to as the space-time sandwich. Imagine time as how many steps a machine takes to solve a problem, while space is about how many distinct tape cells it uses during that process. The key idea here is captured by Sipser's sandwich theorem, which tells us about the inclusion of these resources.

So, for any function f(n) that grows at least as fast as the logarithm of n, we find that if a machine runs in f(n) time, it can only use up to f(n) space. That’s the first part of the sandwich: TIME(f(n)) is included in SPACE(f(n)). Now, if we look at the other side, any space usage of s(n) cells implies that the machine can have at most 2 raised to the order of s(n) distinct configurations. If it revisits a configuration, it must loop. Hence, it can't run forever and will complete in at most 2 raised to the order of s(n) time. 

This gives us the second part of our sandwich: SPACE(f(n)) is included in TIME(2 raised to the order of f(n)). This relationship helps us understand complexity classes like P, PSPACE, and EXPTIME, showing how they fit into the hierarchy based on these inclusions.
