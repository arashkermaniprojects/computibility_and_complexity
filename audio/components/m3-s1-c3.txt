In this notation, we see a sequence of computation histories represented as C1, C2, C3, and so on, up to C subscript ℓ. Each C represents a snapshot of the state of a computation at a specific point in time, separated by the hash symbol, which acts as a delimiter. This structure allows us to track the evolution of a computation step by step. For example, C1 might represent the initial configuration of a Turing machine, while C2 shows the next state after one transition, and C3 captures yet another stage. This method of concatenating computation histories is crucial in understanding how recursive functions can refer back to their previous states, illustrating the power of the recursion theorem. The key takeaway here is that tracking computation history is essential for analyzing recursive processes.
