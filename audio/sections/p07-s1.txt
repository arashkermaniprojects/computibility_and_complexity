Understanding whether a program's language can be described by a regular expression is a fundamental question in computer science. It’s appealing because regular languages can be processed really efficiently—think linear-time matchers, which means they can handle input quickly without the need for complex structures like stacks or tapes. If we could decide this question, it would open up a lot of possibilities for optimizing programs and understanding their behavior.

However, the reality is that this question is undecidable in general. What this means is that there's no single algorithm that can answer it for all possible programs. This ties into Rice's theorem, a key concept in computability theory that tells us about properties of languages recognized by Turing machines. When we prove that the set of Turing machines that accept regular languages is undecidable, we’re uncovering one of the simplest yet profound examples of how certain structural properties of languages can’t be easily categorized. This underscores the limits of what we can algorithmically determine about computation and helps frame our understanding of more complex language properties moving forward.
