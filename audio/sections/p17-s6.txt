Let’s dive into some key questions about growth rates and complexity. First, why do we use a log scale on the y-axis? Well, if we plotted 2 to the power of n on a linear scale, by the time n hits 40, it’s around a trillion—while n squared is only 1600. On a linear scale, all those polynomials would basically flatten out into a single line, making it tough to see the differences. The log scale helps us visualize those differences clearly, treating constant factors as shifts upwards, just like Big-O notation does.

Now, when does 2 to the power of n overtake n cubed? That happens around n equals 10. At that point, both are close—1024 for 2 to the power of n and 1000 for n cubed. After that, 2 to the power of n doubles with each step, while n cubed grows at a rate that gets closer to 1. Exponential growth wins out decisively here.

Why is the polynomial versus exponential boundary so crucial? It relates to the Cobham–Edmonds thesis, which suggests polynomial time is consistent across different models and behaves well under composition. Exponential time, on the other hand, can explode, and its efficiency depends heavily on the model you’re using. 

Finally, is n to the power of 100 feasible? Theoretically, yes, it's in polynomial time. But practically, even n equals 2 leads to an unmanageable number of steps. Most problems we find in P have algorithms with much smaller exponents, but there are exceptions, like some interior-point methods for linear programming, which started at n to the power of 3.5 but have improved to n raised to 2 plus a small epsilon.
