Let’s dive into Big-O notation. When we say that a function f(n) is O(g(n)), we're saying that f is eventually upper-bounded by a constant multiple of g. To break that down, we need a couple of constants: one is c, which can be any positive number, and the other is n zero, which is some starting point. So, for all n greater than or equal to n zero, f(n) will always be less than or equal to c times g(n).

Now, it’s important to understand that this constant c doesn’t limit us. For instance, both 1000n squared and n squared fall under O(n squared). They’re in the same class because that multiplicative factor doesn’t change the essence of growth.

Also, we only care about behavior for large n. Any quirky behavior for small n doesn’t affect our classification. 

There are also related notations. If we talk about Ω(g), that gives us a lower bound, meaning f is greater than or equal to c times g for large n. And then there’s Θ(g), which means f is both O(g) and Ω(g) at the same time, giving us a tight bound. Lastly, there’s little o notation, where f is strictly upper-bounded by g, meaning f divided by g approaches zero as n grows. Understanding these relationships helps us analyze algorithms effectively.
