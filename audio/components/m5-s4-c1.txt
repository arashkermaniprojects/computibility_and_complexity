The NP-completeness family tree is rooted at SAT. Cook and Levin proved independently in nineteen seventy-one that SAT is N P hard — every N P language reduces to SAT in polynomial time. From SAT we branch downward. The first hop is to three-SAT, the restricted form where every clause has exactly three literals. From three-SAT we branch out to a layer of classic combinatorial problems: CLIQUE, SUBSET-SUM, the Hamiltonian path problem, three-coloring, and partition. Each arrow on this diagram represents a published polynomial-time reduction. Once we know SAT reduces to three-SAT, and three-SAT reduces to CLIQUE, we automatically know SAT reduces to CLIQUE — reductions chain. So every node here is N P hard. Karp's nineteen seventy-two paper added twenty-one such reductions in a single stroke, opening the floodgates. Today the family tree has hundreds of nodes covering every corner of computer science: graph theory, number theory, scheduling, logic, geometry. They all stand or fall together — if any single one of them turns out to be in P, then P equals N P.
