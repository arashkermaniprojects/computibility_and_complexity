The hierarchy theorem demonstrates a crucial concept in computational complexity. It shows that for any space-bound function, there are problems that require more space than a given class of machines. Imagine we have a list of machines, each one represented by M1, M2, M3, and so on, that can operate within a certain space limit, SPACE(f). We construct a new machine, D, that intentionally disagrees with each machine at the column corresponding to its index. This means D cannot be part of SPACE(f) because it contradicts every machine at its own description. However, D can fit within a slightly larger space, SPACE(f times log f). This process of diagonalization effectively proves the space hierarchy theorem. The takeaway is that more space allows for the computation of more complex problems.
