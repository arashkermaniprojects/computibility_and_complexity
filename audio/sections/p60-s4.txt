Let’s dive into some key questions about regex equivalence and its complexity. First, why is plain regex equivalence in PSPACE? The idea is to convert each regex into a non-deterministic finite automaton, or NFA, using Thompson's construction. This gives us a polynomial increase in size. Now, checking for equivalence boils down to seeing if the symmetric difference between the two NFAs is empty. To do this, we determinize the NFA using subset construction, which may lead to an exponential number of states, but we only need polynomial workspace to handle it. Checking emptiness then uses space proportional to the number of states.

Next, what happens when we add squaring? When you square a regex, it effectively doubles the length of the language it describes. If you keep iterating this squaring, you create languages that grow exponentially from a polynomial-sized regex. This complexity forces the equivalence check into exponential space, making it EXPSPACE-complete, as shown by Stockmeyer and Meyer in 1973.

Practically speaking, this means real-world regex engines steer clear of squaring and back-references. POSIX regex stays manageable, while regex engines in Perl, Java, or Python that support back-references can face NP-hard matching difficulties. In extreme cases, you might encounter catastrophic backtracking, where simple inputs can lead to absurdly long processing times.

Finally, where does EXPSPACE fit into the complexity hierarchy? EXPSPACE includes all problems solvable in exponential space and is defined as the union over k of SPACE(2 raised to n raised to k). It sits above PSPACE and EXPTIME, but below 2-EXPTIME, showing a clear separation in the hierarchy of complexity classes.
