Let’s dive into some key questions about verifiers and brute-force solvers. 

First, why does a verifier finish in m steps instead of 2 to the n? The key here is that the verifier is given a satisfying assignment upfront. Instead of searching through possibilities, it just checks if this assignment satisfies the m clauses—doing one constant-time check for each clause. So, we end up with a total of O(m) steps. 

Now, what if the verifier gets a wrong certificate? In that case, at least one clause is going to evaluate to false, which means the verifier will simply reject it. This highlights an important aspect of NP: it's one-sided. There exists at least one valid certificate for a yes-instance, but the verifier only needs to recognize the good ones, not all possible ones.

Next, let’s touch on UNSAT, the complement of SAT. UNSAT is thought not to be in NP because to certify that no assignment works, you’d need to rule out all 2 to the n possibilities, and there’s no known polynomial-size certificate for that. Instead, UNSAT is in coNP, and whether NP equals coNP is still an open question.

Can our solver be smarter than brute force? Absolutely! Techniques like DPLL and CDCL run faster in practice. But, we don’t have any algorithm with a known polynomial worst-case runtime; the worst-case remains 2 to the O(n).

Finally, look at PRIMES. For a long time, it was believed to be only in NP. But in 2002, it was proven to be in P, meaning there is a polynomial-time algorithm for it. So being "in NP" doesn’t imply there’s no polynomial algorithm; it just means one isn’t obvious.
