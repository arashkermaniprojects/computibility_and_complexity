Let’s dive into some key questions about the relationship between time and space in algorithms. First, why isn't time equal to space? Well, think about it this way: space can be reused, but time can't. If you have a machine that's limited to a certain amount of space, it can revisit the same memory cells many times, potentially an exponential number of times, before it either halts or gets stuck in a loop. But once you take a step in time, that step is gone forever.

Now, let’s look at some real examples where space is cheap but time is expensive. Consider depth-first search with an explicit stack. It uses O(depth) space, but in the worst case, it might have to explore exponentially many leaves. Other algorithms like branch-and-bound, alpha-beta pruning, and recursive backtracking also tend to use polynomial space while facing exponential running times.

Next, is the bound that says SPACE is a subset of TIME with exponential growth tight? In a relativized world, yes. There are oracles that separate PSPACE from EXPTIME. The question of whether PSPACE equals EXPTIME or is just a subset of it is still open.

Finally, where does NL fit into all this? NL, which stands for nondeterministic logarithmic space, is equal to NSPACE of log n. It's contained within P due to Savitch's theorem and deterministic simulation. It also trivially fits within PSPACE, and interestingly, NL equals coNL, thanks to the Immerman-Szelepcsényi theorem.
