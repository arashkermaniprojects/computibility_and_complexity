In Generalized Geography, we can understand why the problem is PSPACE-complete by looking at how it relates to TQBF, or true quantified Boolean formulas. Each formula can be transformed into a geography graph. In this graph, one player, called the existential player, picks vertices that correspond to existential variables, while the other player, the universal player, chooses for universal variables. This setup creates a game where both players must make decisions that lead to a play tree. The depth of this tree is polynomially related to the size of the formula, meaning that the game can explore a vast number of possibilities within a bounded space. The key takeaway here is that the complexity of Generalized Geography encapsulates the intricate decision-making processes found in TQBF, highlighting its PSPACE-complete nature.
