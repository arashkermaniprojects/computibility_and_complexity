Let’s dive into two important languages related to linear-bounded automata, or LBAs. First, we have A sub L B A, which consists of pairs like ⟨M, w⟩. Here, M is an LBA and w is an input string. The question is whether M accepts w. This turns out to be decidable. There’s a clear method to figure it out: you can simulate the LBA on the input, checking its configurations since the computation is bounded by the length of w. So, we can systematically explore all possible configurations until we find an answer.

Now, contrast that with E sub L B A. This language involves pairs like ⟨M⟩, where M is an LBA, and the question is whether the language accepted by M is empty—that is, L(M) = ∅. This problem is undecidable. The reason lies in how we think about computations. Instead of just bounded configurations, we need to consider the whole history of the computation, which can get really complex. This complexity makes it impossible to find a general method for determining emptiness for all LBAs. So, while one question is solvable, the other isn't, highlighting an interesting aspect of computational theory.
