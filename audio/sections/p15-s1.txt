Let’s talk about the fixed-point form of the recursion theorem. This theorem tells us something fascinating about computable functions. Imagine you have a computable total function, which we can think of as a program transformation, let’s call it t. What the theorem states is that for every such function t, there exists a Turing machine, which we’ll call R, that behaves in a very special way. Specifically, R recognizes the same language as the output of t when you feed it the description of R itself.

Now, this is powerful because it’s saying R is a fixed point of t. What that means is that while R and t applied to the description of R might look different in terms of their actual code, they recognize the same set of strings. So, R and t(⟨R⟩) are not identical machines, but they behave the same regarding what languages they recognize.

This idea leads us to some interesting implications. For instance, quines, which are programs that print themselves, are a specific case of this theorem. The essence here is about programs being able to reason about themselves, and that’s a key concept in theoretical computer science.
