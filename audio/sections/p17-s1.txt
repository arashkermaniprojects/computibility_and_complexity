Let’s dive into the asymptotic hierarchy and understand how different growth rates affect complexity. When we talk about complexity, we’re really focusing on how the time it takes to solve a problem grows as the size of the input increases, rather than how fast a particular algorithm runs in real-world terms. 

Imagine six different functions racing against each other: log n, n, n log n, n squared, n cubed, and 2 to the power of n. At smaller input sizes, like n = 40, you might notice that log n and n still seem quite manageable, producing values around 5 and 40 respectively. But as n grows, things change dramatically. By the time we reach n = 40, n squared already hits 1,600, and n cubed jumps to 64,000. 

Now, consider 2 to the power of n. At n = 40, it’s around a trillion! The gap between n cubed and 2 to the power of n at this point is about fifteen million-fold. As we push n to 60 or 100, that gap widens even more. By n = 100, 2 to the power of n is so vast that even the heat death of the universe wouldn’t witness the end of those operations. This clearly illustrates how exponential growth can quickly overtake polynomial growth, highlighting the importance of understanding these rates in algorithm design.
