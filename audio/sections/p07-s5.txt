Let’s consider a specific example with a Turing machine, M, that always halts and rejects every input. No matter what you give it, it will never accept. So, for any input w, M(w) will always be reject. This means that when we look at our construction for L(M'), we find that it ends up being non-regular, specifically the language {0^n 1^n}. Since M never accepts any input, we know that branch two of our construction will never accept either. This tells us that the encoding of M', which represents this non-regular language, does not belong to the class of regular Turing machines.

Now, let’s switch gears and look at a different machine, M', which always halts and accepts every possible input. For any input w, M' will accept it. In this case, since M accepts everything, branch two will always lead to an acceptance as well. Here, L(M') becomes the entire set of strings over our alphabet, which is regular. So, in this scenario, the encoding of M', or ⟨M'⟩, does indeed belong to REGULAR TM. This contrast between the two machines helps illustrate why REGULAR TM is undecidable.
