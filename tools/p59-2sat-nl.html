2-SAT ∈ NL · implication-graph trick

A 2-CNF formula is unsatisfiable iff some variable z has both z → ¬z and ¬z → z in the implication graph.

1 · Theory
Theorem · 2-SAT ∈ NL

Each 2-clause (a ∨ b) is equivalent to two implications: ¬a → b and ¬b → a. UNSAT iff some var z has paths z → ¬z and ¬z → z. Use NL = coNL: a coNL machine guesses no such cycle exists.

3 · Pitfalls
  • The implication-graph trick is specific to 2-clauses. A k-clause for k ≥ 3 has too much flexibility — multiple implications combine non-locally. Why 2-SAT is in P but 3-SAT is NP-complete.
  • 2-SAT is in NL via NL = coNL. UNSAT is in NL (Immerman–Szelepcsényi). Both 2-SAT and ¬2-SAT in NL.
  • SAT-decision and SAT-search coincide for 2-SAT. Both are in P (and NL); the deterministic algorithm gives an assignment, not just yes/no.
4 · Q&A defence notes
  • Q1. Why does 2-SAT have a smart algorithm but not 3-SAT?
    A 2-clause (a ∨ b) decomposes into exactly two implications: ¬a → b and ¬b → a. The implication structure is finite-tree-like. A 3-clause (a ∨ b ∨ c) has 3 × 2 = 6 partial implications that don't combine cleanly. The graph trick breaks down.
  • Q2. Why NL and not L?
    PATH in the implication graph is checked nondeterministically (Problem 42). If we could check directed graph PATH in L, then L = NL — currently open.
  • Q3. Is 2-SAT in P?
    Yes — via SCC computation in O(V + E) using Tarjan or Kosaraju. The SCC algorithm gives both decision and assignment. NL ⊆ P, consistent with 2-SAT ∈ NL.
  • Q4. What about Horn-SAT?
    Horn-SAT (clauses with at most one positive literal) is also in P, by unit propagation. Linear-time. Both 2-SAT and Horn-SAT are "easy SAT" cases below the NP-complete general SAT.
