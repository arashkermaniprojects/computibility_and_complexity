Closure properties · Decidable vs Recognizable

Decidable is closed under everything. Recognizable is NOT closed under complement — and that gap is the bridge theorem.

CLO-1R1·30 ptsL5
1 · Closure properties · what each class is stable under

"Closed under operation ⊙" means: if L1, L2 are in the class, then L1 ⊙ L2 is also in the class. Closure properties define what the class lets us do with its members.

Decidable languages · closed under everything

Decidable is closed under union (∪), intersection (∩), complement (¬), concatenation (·), and Kleene star (*). Easy: build a decider that runs the constituent deciders and combines their always-halting outputs.

Recognizable languages · closed under almost everything except complement

Recognizable is closed under ∪ (parallel simulation), ∩ (sequential or parallel simulation), concatenation, and star. But NOT closed under complement. ATM is recognizable; its complement co-ATM is not — that's the heart of the asymmetry.

The bridge theorem

L is decidable ⇔ both L and L̅ are recognizable.

The bridge theorem ties the two classes together. To decide L: run recognisers for L and L̅ in parallel; exactly one halts on any input. The non-closure of recognisable under complement is exactly what makes some recognisable languages undecidable.

3 · Pitfalls
  • "Recognizable" allows looping; "decidable" doesn't. The distinction is exactly halting behaviour on non-members. A decider always halts; a recogniser may loop on x ∉ L.
  • Parallel simulation isn't sequential. To recognise L1 ∪ L2, you can't run M1 to completion then M2 — M1 might loop. Run them in interleaved steps so each makes progress.
  • The complement closure failure is the whole story. Almost every undecidability proof exploits "L is recognizable but L̅ is not." The bridge theorem makes this explicit.
4 · Q&A defence notes
  • Q1. Why is recognisable not closed under complement?
    A recogniser for L may loop on x ∉ L. To recognise ¬L, we'd want to accept x ∉ L, which means detecting "the original recogniser loops on x" — equivalent to the halting problem, which is undecidable. So no general construction exists.
  • Q2. Why is recognisable closed under union (the recogniser doesn't just "run both" sequentially)?
    It does — but in parallel, not in sequence. Simulate M1 and M2 with interleaved steps; accept the moment either accepts. Sequential simulation fails because M1 might loop forever, leaving M2 never reached.
  • Q3. Prove the bridge theorem.
    (⇒) Decidable ⇒ recogniser (just the decider itself); decidable is closed under complement, so L̅ is also decidable, hence recognisable. (⇐) Both L and L̅ have recognisers M1, M2. Simulate both in parallel on any input x. Exactly one accepts (x is in L or in L̅, not both). Halt with the answer.
  • Q4. Are regular and context-free languages closed under different operations?
    Regular: closed under everything (∪, ∩, ¬, ·, *, intersection with regular). CFL: closed under ∪, ·, *, intersection with regular — but not complement, not intersection with CFL.
