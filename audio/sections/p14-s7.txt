Alright, let’s dive into some common pitfalls students encounter when learning about Rice's theorem. First off, it’s crucial to understand that Rice's theorem states that nontrivial semantic properties of Turing machines are undecidable. But remember, not all properties are nontrivial. Some syntactic or trivial properties can still be decidable. 

A common mix-up is between "M halts on w" and "w is in L(M)." These are two different statements. When we say "M halts," it includes cases where M might reject or just not halt at all. On the other hand, "w in L(M)" specifically means that M accepts w. Both of these statements lead to undecidable problems, but they are not the same thing. 

Also, when determining if a property P is nontrivial, you need to check both sides. If every recognizable language satisfies P, like "L(M) is recognizable," then it’s trivial. And don’t forget about the direction of your reductions. You should be reducing from A sub T M to L sub P, not the other way around. Finally, while the recursion theorem can provide a shorter proof for some results, it’s not required for the reduction proofs we’re discussing. Keep these distinctions clear, and you'll have a much better grasp of the concepts!
