Let’s dive into the reduction graph and how it connects various undecidable problems. When we look at the reduction from A sub T M to HALT T M, we first create a new machine, M prime, that accepts only a specific input, w. This technique helps us demonstrate that if we could solve HALT T M, we could also solve A sub T M. 

This idea doesn’t stop with HALT T M. We can also show that problems like regular Turing machines, context-free languages, and even the equality problem for Turing machines are undecidable through similar reductions. For instance, when we say the equality problem is not recognizable or co-recognizable, we mean that there’s no way to determine if two machines accept the same language or not.

Then, we have more complex problems like Post Correspondence Problem, tiling problems, and the word problem for groups. All of these can be reduced from either A sub T M or HALT T M, reinforcing their undecidability. 

A key concept is Rice’s theorem, which tells us that any non-trivial property of a language will reduce from A sub T M. Each of these proofs follows a similar structure: if you could solve one problem, you could solve A sub T M, which we know is impossible. Understanding these connections helps solidify the landscape of undecidable problems and the power of reductions in computability theory.
