Let’s dive into why satisfiability is preserved in both directions when we transform a formula to have clauses with exactly three literals. First, if our original formula φ is satisfiable, we need to show that the transformed version remains satisfiable. Suppose we take a clause with more than three literals. There’s at least one literal that’s true under our assignment. As we move through the clause, we can introduce auxiliary variables to help maintain the chain of satisfaction. We set those auxiliary variables to true until we reach our true literal, and then we can set the remaining ones to false. This ensures that every chain-clause and the original clause is satisfied.

Now, let’s consider the reverse direction. If the transformed formula φ' is satisfied, we want to show that the original formula φ is also satisfied. Assume for a moment that all literals in the original clause are false. This would create a situation where the first chain-clause forces an auxiliary variable to be true, and then through propagation, we find that all auxiliary variables become true. However, this leads to a contradiction because the last chain-clause must also be satisfied, which isn't possible if all literals are false. Therefore, at least one literal in the original clause must indeed be true, preserving satisfiability.
