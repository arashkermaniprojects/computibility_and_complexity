Let’s talk about some important pitfalls when dealing with composites and the notion of divisors. First off, when we say "polynomial," we mean polynomial in terms of the bit length of the number, not just the number itself. The bit length of a number n is roughly the logarithm of n, which means it’s actually a lot smaller than n. So when we’re looking for a divisor, we want to consider that the bit length of a divisor is about half the log of n, which keeps it polynomial.

Now, we need to be careful about trivial divisors. The numbers 1 and n are considered trivial and don’t really help us in this context. We want a non-trivial divisor that lies strictly between 1 and n. If we didn’t have this restriction, any number could be said to have a divisor, and that would make the whole problem trivial. 

Now, let’s connect this to primes and composites. Primes are basically the numbers that don’t have divisors other than 1 and themselves, while composites do. Both primes and composites belong to NP, but they also fall under P, thanks to breakthroughs like the AKS algorithm in 2002. For many years, we only knew they were in NP, so this was a big deal. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for grasping the complexities of number theory and computational classes.
