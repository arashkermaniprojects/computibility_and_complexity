Let’s dive into why problems like PATH, RELPRIME, and ACFG are considered in P, while their NP-hard cousins are so much more challenging. The key distinction often comes down to how we frame the problem, particularly with quantifiers. 

Take the PATH problem: it simply asks if there’s a path between two points in a graph. This is straightforward because we can efficiently explore the graph and find that answer. Now, compare that to HAMPATH, which is about finding a path that visits every vertex exactly once. This turns combinatorial, meaning the number of possible paths grows exponentially, and checking each one is not feasible.

Similarly, think about deterministic finite automata, or DFAs. They can simulate a machine in a clear, structured way, which is easy to compute. But when we look at SAT, the problem of finding an assignment to variables that satisfies all clauses, it’s a different story altogether. We’re not just checking one path—we're trying to navigate through a maze of possibilities, making the task much harder. 

So, the difference here is about complexity and the nature of what you’re trying to find. It's that subtle shift in how we define the problem that can take us from a manageable task to one that’s NP-hard.
