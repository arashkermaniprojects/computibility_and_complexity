Here you see two tapes side by side. On top is an ordinary Turing machine. Its tape starts with the input — here the symbols a b b a a, length 5 — and extends to the right forever with blank cells. The head can wander as far as it wants, writing as it goes. That unboundedness is exactly why the number of possible configurations is infinite, and why A sub T M is only recognizable, not decidable.

Below is a linear bounded automaton, or L B A. The crucial change is the two gold end markers, written left-tack and right-tack. The L B A reads them like normal symbols but is forbidden to move past them. So the head is trapped inside exactly n cells, where n is the input length. It can read and rewrite those cells, but it cannot stretch.

Why does this matter? With only n cells and a finite tape alphabet, the L B A has at most number-of-states times n times gamma-to-the-n distinct configurations — finite, even if astronomically large. So if you simulate the L B A for that many steps and it still hasn't halted, it must have repeated a configuration, which means it loops forever. You reject. This is Sipser theorem 5.9: A sub L B A is decidable. Same machine model, finite tape budget, decidable membership.
