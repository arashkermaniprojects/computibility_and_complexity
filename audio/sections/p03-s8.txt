Let’s dive into some key Q&A notes to solidify our understanding. First, why is D itself a Turing machine? Well, D is built from H using composition. When you run H and flip the result, you're still working within the realm of Turing machines. Since H is a Turing machine, D inherits that property too. Remember, every effectively-computable procedure is a Turing machine, according to the Church–Turing thesis.

Now, let’s talk about that diagonal cell. It creates a contradiction because it’s the unique cell where D’s input matches its own description. In that scenario, D is forced to disagree with itself. 

Now, think about Cantor's diagonal argument. It has a similar structure. Cantor’s method shows disagreement with function values at specific indices. In our case, we disagree with the Turing machine at the index where it describes itself.

Next, why doesn’t this contradiction affect the universal Turing machine? The universal Turing machine is a recognizer, not a decider. Recognizers can loop indefinitely. The contradiction in D relies on H being a decider, which always halts.

As a bonus question, is A sub T M co-recognizable? The answer is no. If it were, combining the recognizer and the co-recognizer would let us decide A sub T M. Since A sub T M is undecidable, its complement cannot be recognizable either.
