Let’s dive into some important pitfalls when working with our single-tape Turing machine. First, remember that the order of symbols matters significantly. We need to ensure that our input strictly follows the pattern of 0s followed by 1s, like in the sequence 0ⁿ1ⁿ. Inputs such as "1100" or "010101" are not valid—those patterns are interleaved or just out of order, and we need to reject them.

To handle this, we start with a structural scan. This first pass checks that the input conforms to the 0*1* shape before we even begin pairing. Once we confirm that, we can proceed to pair the 0s and 1s. Each pairing takes linear time, but remember that we also have to account for the time spent rewinding the tape. In fact, this rewind contributes to the overall complexity. The constant factor here is about 3: you walk right to find a 1, then rewind all the way back, and start the next pairing.

When we incorporate these rewinds, it’s what pushes our lower bound to Θ(n²), rather than just O(n²). And don’t forget about marking! We’re using new symbols, X and Y, to represent consumed 0s and 1s. These symbols need to be included in our tape alphabet, as they’re separate from 0 and 1 and help us manage our computations without destroying the original input.
