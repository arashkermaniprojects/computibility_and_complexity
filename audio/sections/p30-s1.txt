Let’s dive into the gadget reduction from 3SAT to HAMPATH, focusing on the diamonds and spreaders. Imagine we have a variable, say x sub i. For each variable, we create a “diamond” structure. Picture it as a long row of nodes where you can only traverse from left to right or right to left. If you go left to right, it means x sub i is true. Going right to left indicates x sub i is false. You can't mix these paths; it’s a strict rule.

Now, for each clause in our 3SAT formula, we introduce what's called a spreader. This consists of a clause node, let’s say c sub j, which connects to specific positions in the diamonds of the variables involved in that clause. If x sub i appears positively in the clause, c sub j connects to the left-to-right edge of x sub i’s diamond. Conversely, if ¬x sub i is present, it connects to the right-to-left edge. 

When we’re looking for a Hamiltonian path, it has to touch every clause node c sub j. The only way this is possible is by using edges that are available based on the truth assignments of the literals. This means that for the Hamiltonian path to exist, every clause must have at least one true literal. That’s the essence of how we connect the satisfiability of the clauses to the Hamiltonian paths in our graph.
