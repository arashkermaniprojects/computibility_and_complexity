Savitch's theorem reveals a fascinating relationship between nondeterministic and deterministic space complexity. Imagine a recursion tree where each level corresponds to a step in a computation. The depth of this tree is logarithmic in the size of the input, specifically log t, where t is the time taken by the nondeterministic machine. Each frame of this tree uses O(f) bits of space. When you multiply the depth, log t, by the space per frame, O(f), you find that the total space used is O(f squared). 

Even though the tree has exponentially many leaves, representing all possible computation paths, only one path is active on the call stack at any time. This leads us to conclude that nondeterministic space complexity is indeed contained within deterministic space complexity squared. The key takeaway is that nondeterministic space can be efficiently simulated with deterministic space that grows quadratically.
