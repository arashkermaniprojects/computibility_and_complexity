Let’s dive into the concept of complement-graph duality. Picture a graph, G, with vertices and edges. A vertex cover is a set of vertices that touches every edge. So, for every edge connecting two vertices, at least one of those vertices must be included in your vertex cover. Now, when we talk about the complement graph, G̅, we’re flipping the edges. If there’s an edge between two vertices in G, there is no edge between them in G̅, and vice versa.

Now, here’s the key point: if you have a clique of size k in G, meaning that every pair of vertices in that clique is connected by an edge, then in the complement graph G̅, no pair of those vertices shares an edge. This implies that the vertices not in the clique, which we call V minus S, form a vertex cover in G̅. They cover all the edges in G̅ because every edge has at least one endpoint outside of that clique.

This relationship leads us to a powerful reduction. If you can find a clique in G, you can determine a vertex cover in G̅ and vice versa. This duality not only helps in proving the NP-completeness of these problems but also gives us a deeper understanding of their structure and interconnections.
