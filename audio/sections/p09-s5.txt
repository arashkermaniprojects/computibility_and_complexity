Let's dive into some key questions and answers about Turing machines and their computation histories. First, why do we insert the state inside the tape? A configuration needs to capture the tape contents, the head position, and the current state all in one string. By placing the state symbol right before the head cell, we can compactly encode all three elements. This setup allows us to directly see how the head moves between configurations, as the state symbol will shift left or right from one configuration to the next.

Next, why is local checkability so powerful? It acts as a bridge between Turing machine power and simpler models like linear bounded automata and context-free grammars. While these simpler models can verify a small 2×3 window, they can't perform global counting. Local checking is within their abilities, but simulating a full Turing machine isn't.

Now, what about the decidability of A sub T M? The trick we discussed doesn’t make it decidable because the verification process moves outward, not inward. We’re actually reducing A sub T M to questions about LBAs or CFGs. Since A sub T M is undecidable, that undecidability carries over to our simpler questions.

If M loops on input w, what happens? In that case, there's no finite accepting computation history. The target language in our reduction is empty, which gives us exactly the information we need: if M loops or rejects on w, then the LBA accepts no history.

Lastly, could we use a different separator instead of the pound sign? Absolutely! Any symbol not found in the set of tape symbols or states works. The pound sign is just a convention because it looks clean and neat, but it doesn't change the underlying construction.
