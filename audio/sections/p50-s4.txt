Let’s dive into the Q&A defense notes. First, you might wonder why we choose a fixed finite chunk like the set where the length of x is less than or equal to 10. The reason is simple: it guarantees that the language of our new machine, M', is non-empty in both scenarios, but it turns finite or infinite based on whether M accepts w. And yes, that “10” is arbitrary. Any explicitly finite set will work just fine.

Now, could we use any finite set? Absolutely! You could use {ε}, {0, 1}, or really any finite set that's clearly enumerable. What’s important is that we can decide if the set is finite or not independently of M.

Next, let’s look at Rice's theorem. It tells us that if we say “L(M) is finite,” that depends solely on the language itself, which is a semantic property. It doesn’t matter how M is built, and it’s nontrivial because some Turing machines have finite languages while others do not. So, FINITE TM is undecidable, and we don’t need any complex construction beyond Rice.

Lastly, regarding regular Turing machines, there’s a parallel. The set REGULAR TM, which is the collection of machines where L(M) is regular, is also undecidable by Rice's theorem. Regularity is also a semantic property and nontrivial, so we can use a similar construction to show that it’s undecidable.
