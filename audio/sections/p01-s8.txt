Let’s dive into some questions about Cantor's diagonal argument. First, why is the flipped function g not in the list? Well, g disagrees with the function in row i at column i minus one for every i. So, it can’t be the same as any function in the list, making it unique. 

Now, why does Cantor's theorem matter for computability? Remember, Turing machines can only be countably many because each has a finite description. The languages they recognize, however, form a much larger, uncountable set. This means there are way more languages than Turing machines can handle, indicating that most languages aren't Turing-recognizable.

Next, is this the same as the A sub T M diagonalization problem? The technique is similar, but the context differs. In this case, we’re working with arbitrary functions, while in the A sub T M problem, we’re flipping accepts and rejects for Turing machines.

Does the order of listing matter? Not at all! We just need any enumeration of functions. The diagonal trick works for any such listing.

Lastly, let’s prove that the reals between 0 and 1 are uncountable. Each real number in that interval has a binary expansion, which can be thought of as a function from natural numbers to {0, 1}. This shows that the size of the interval is the same as the size of the set of all functions from natural numbers to {0, 1}. So, Cantor's theorem applies here as well! Just keep in mind the caveat about dyadic numbers; they have two expansions but don’t affect the overall proof.
