Let’s dive into some important questions about recognizable languages. First, why isn’t recognizable closed under complement? Imagine you have a recognizer for a language L. If you give it an input that’s not in L, it might just loop forever, never giving you a clear answer. To create a recognizer for the complement, you’d need to determine whether that original recognizer is looping. But that’s basically the halting problem, which we know is undecidable. So, there’s no general way to construct a recognizer for complements.

Now, let’s talk about unions. Recognizable languages are closed under union, but it’s not just a matter of running both recognizers one after the other. Instead, you simulate both recognizers at the same time, interleaving their steps. If either one accepts, you accept the input. If you tried to do this sequentially, one recognizer could loop forever, and you’d never get to the other one.

Moving on to the bridge theorem, it states that if a language is decidable, it’s also recognizable. The decider itself can serve as a recognizer. Since decidable languages are closed under complement, both the language and its complement are recognizable. Conversely, if you have recognizers for both a language and its complement, you can run them in parallel. Since one must accept, you can confidently halt and give the correct answer.

Finally, let’s look at regular and context-free languages. Regular languages are closed under all operations: union, intersection, complement, concatenation, and even intersection with other regular languages. Context-free languages are a bit more limited; they’re closed under union, concatenation, and Kleene star, but they don’t handle complements or intersections with other context-free languages well.
