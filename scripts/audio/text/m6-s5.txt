Zooming out: the big map at this point. P is inside N-P is inside P-S-P-A-C-E, and P-S-P-A-C-E equals N-P-S-P-A-C-E by Savitch. P-S-P-A-C-E is inside E-X-P- T-I-M-E. We know P is strictly inside E-X-P-T-I-M-E by the time hierarchy theorem, which we will see in the next module. So at least one of the containments P inside N-P, N-P inside P-S-P-A-C-E, P-S-P-A-C-E inside E-X-P- T-I-M-E must be strict — but we cannot tell you which one. This is one of the most striking gaps in our understanding of computation. The widget visualizes this nested-circles picture, with classic problems placed at the right level. N-P-complete problems sit at the boundary of N-P; P-S-P-A-C-E-complete problems sit at the boundary of P-S-P-A-C-E. Both kinds of completeness give us proxy measures of how hard a class actually is.
