SAT, the satisfiability problem, asks whether a given Boolean formula has some assignment of true and false to its variables that makes the whole formula evaluate to true. Try the controls: pick one of three preset formulas, then click on any variable circle to flip it between true and false. Watch the clauses turn green when they are satisfied and red when they are not. A formula is satisfiable when you can color all the clauses green simultaneously. The middle preset is intentionally impossible — no assignment satisfies all four clauses, so the formula is unsatisfiable. The auto-solve button runs the obvious brute-force algorithm: try every assignment in turn until one works or all fail. With only three variables we have just eight assignments, but the worst case grows as two to the n. Three-SAT is the same problem with the extra requirement that every clause has exactly three literals. The restriction does not make it any easier — three-SAT remains N P complete — but the uniform structure makes it the standard launching pad for most reductions to other N P-complete problems.
