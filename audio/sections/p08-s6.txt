When working on mapping reductions, especially when dealing with the undecidability of ¬EQ TM, students often trip over a few key points. First, remember that we're reducing to ¬EQ TM, not EQ TM. So when you take an instance like ⟨M, w⟩ from A sub T M, it corresponds to ⟨M1, M2⟩ that is not in EQ TM. This is crucial because the undecidability of ¬EQ TM directly tells us that EQ TM is undecidable as well. 

Now, let’s talk about M2—it’s designed to ignore its input. A common mistake is to say, "M2 on input x runs M on x." That won't work because you’d find that L(M2) equals L(M), which isn't what we want. M2 should ignore its input completely and always check M against the specific string w. Also, be careful with the concept of loops. If M runs forever on w, that means M2, regardless of input, will also run forever on w. So, in that case, L(M2) becomes empty. 

Remember, when constructing M2, you're not actually running M; you're describing how it would simulate M on w. The real action happens later, only if the EQ TM oracle decides to call M2. Keep these points in mind, and you'll avoid some common pitfalls!
