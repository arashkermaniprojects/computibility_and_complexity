The N P completeness family tree is rooted at sat. Cook and Levin proved independently in nineteen seventy-one that sat is N P hard — every N P language reduces to sat in polynomial time. From sat we branch downward. The first hop is to three-sat, the restricted form where every clause has exactly three literals. From three-sat we branch out to a layer of classic combinatorial problems: clique, subset sum, the Hamiltonian path problem, three coloring, and partition. Each arrow on this diagram represents a published polynomial-time reduction. Once we know sat reduces to three-sat, and three-sat reduces to clique, we automatically know sat reduces to clique — reductions chain. So every node here is N P hard. Karp's nineteen seventy-two paper added twenty-one such reductions in a single stroke, opening the floodgates. Today the family tree has hundreds of nodes covering every corner of computer science: graph theory, number theory, scheduling, logic, geometry. They all stand or fall together — if any single one of them turns out to be in P, then P equals N P.
