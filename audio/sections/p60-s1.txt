Let’s dive into the theory behind regular expressions and their equivalence checking, especially when we introduce squaring. Back in 1973, Stockmeyer and Meyer showed that when we add squaring to regular expressions, which we can denote as R squared or R², the problem of checking whether two expressions are equivalent becomes really complex — specifically, it’s classified as EXPSPACE-complete. 

Why is that the case? Imagine you have a Turing machine that operates in exponential space. You can encode its entire computation as a regular expression. This is significant because a regular expression with squaring can express languages that need a minimal deterministic finite automaton, or DFA, that has an exponential size. So, when you're trying to determine if two squaring-enhanced regex patterns describe the same language, you’re essentially grappling with this complexity of exponential growth. This means that the resources needed to solve the equivalence problem can grow exponentially, pushing us into the realm of exponential space. Understanding this relationship helps clarify why squaring elevates the difficulty of equivalence checking for regular expressions.
