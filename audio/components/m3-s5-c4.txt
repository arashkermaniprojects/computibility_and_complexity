Let’s explore the recursion theorem and its implications for undecidability. Suppose we have a hypothetical machine, D, that can decide the acceptance problem for Turing machines, known as A TM. According to the recursion theorem, we can construct a Turing machine, M, that operates as follows: first, it retrieves its own description, denoted as ⟨M⟩. Next, it uses D to determine whether M accepts a given input w by computing D(⟨M, w⟩). However, M then flips the answer it receives from D. This leads to a contradiction: M will accept w if and only if D indicates that M does not accept w. Thus, we see that D cannot exist, reinforcing the undecidability of A TM. The key takeaway is that self-reference in computation reveals inherent limitations in decision problems.
