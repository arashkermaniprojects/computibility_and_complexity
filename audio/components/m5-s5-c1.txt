This is the big picture map of the P versus N P landscape. The yellow outer ellipse is N P — every language with a polynomial-time verifier. The green inner ellipse is P — the languages we can actually decide in polynomial time. P sits comfortably inside N P. Inside P we see well-behaved problems: path, two-coloring of graphs, and primality testing, all decidable in polynomial time. On the far right edge of N P sits the orange chain — the N P complete problems. sat, three-sat, clique, vertex cover, Hamiltonian path, and subset sum form a closed loop of mutually-reducible problems sitting at the maximum hardness within N P. The million-dollar question is: do P and N P actually coincide? Every N P-complete problem is a single polynomial-time algorithm away from answering yes — if we ever find a fast algorithm for any one of them, the entire N P-complete frontier collapses into P. After half a century of effort, no such algorithm has been found, and most experts believe P is strictly smaller than N P. But there is no proof yet, and the question carries a one-million-dollar Clay Institute prize.
