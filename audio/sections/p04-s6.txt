Let’s dive into how different languages fit into our framework of decidability and recognizability. 

First, we have deterministic finite automata, or DFAs. Languages like A sub D F A, E sub D F A, and EQ sub D F A are all decidable. We can simulate their behavior or check graph reachability to determine membership. 

Next, context-free grammars, or CFGs, also have decidable languages like A sub C F G and E sub C F G. We can use methods like the CYK algorithm for parsing or checking emptiness. 

Now, when we move to Turing machines, things get a bit trickier. The languages A sub T M and HALT sub T M fall into the recognizable category but are not decidable. These are handled by the universal Turing machine, and diagonalization shows why we can’t decide them. 

On the other hand, the complements ¬A sub T M and ¬HALT sub T M are co-recognizable, thanks to the bridging theorem. 

For E sub T M, it’s co-recognizable too, while its complement, ¬E sub T M, is recognizable. 

Lastly, EQ sub T M lands in neither category. It’s crucial to understand that all non-trivial properties fall under Rice’s theorem, which shows how complex these languages can be.
