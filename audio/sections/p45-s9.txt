Let’s start by engaging the class with a thought-provoking question: among all those big open problems like P versus NP or L versus NL, is there any separation we can actually prove? That’s where the space hierarchy theorem comes in. 

Now, let’s state the theorem clearly. It tells us that for any function f that grows, there’s a strict separation between languages that can be recognized with space f and those that can be recognized with space f times the logarithm of f. This gap, while small, is non-trivial, and it's our gateway into understanding more complex relationships in computational theory.

Next, let’s dive into how we construct our diagonalization argument. Picture three main steps: we first enumerate all the languages, then we simulate our machine on those languages, and finally, we flip a bit to create a language that’s guaranteed not to be in our original list. 

When we visualize this, pause when you see the row for D highlighted in gold. This is where we find our contradiction. If D is in the list, it means it can be simulated, but that leads us to a situation where b equals one minus b, which just can’t hold true. 

Finally, let’s wrap up by listing some important corollaries: we get separations like L is a subset of PSPACE, NL is a subset of PSPACE, P is a subset of EXPTIME, and NP is a subset of NEXPTIME. It’s crucial to close with a reminder: this hierarchy can’t give us P does not equal NP, since we’re dealing with different types of determinism but the same resource bounds.
