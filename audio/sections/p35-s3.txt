Let’s dive into some important pitfalls when discussing the relationship between NSPACE and SPACE. First off, we need to remember that while we can say NSPACE of f is contained within PSPACE, the upper bound f squared is not necessarily the lower bound. This means we don’t have a definitive answer on whether NSPACE of f is strictly smaller than SPACE of f. It’s an open question, which keeps things interesting!

Now, when we apply Savitch’s theorem, we notice a key detail about the quadratic blowup we prove. While we get this quadratic increase, it might not be absolutely necessary in every case. Another important point is about recursion depth. We typically think of it as log of t, but here it’s actually log of f. When we consider our time budget t, it’s roughly 2 raised to the power of O(f), leading us to log of t being O(f). So, we’re counting the depth based on that.

Finally, don’t forget about CANYIELD in the proof. It’s not part of the actual algorithm but rather a thought-experiment routine. The implementation reconstructs configurations using their O(f)-bit encodings, which is crucial for understanding how we navigate through these states. Keep these nuances in mind as you explore this fascinating topic!
