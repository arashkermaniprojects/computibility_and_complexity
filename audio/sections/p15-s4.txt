Let’s dive into the animated demo that illustrates the fixed-point theorem in action. Imagine a program, which we’ll call P, that behaves in a particular way based on its input. Now, the magic happens when we apply a transformation, q, to P. This transformation essentially creates a new program, P prime, which has a very special property: it can auto-play its own behavior.

As you watch the animation, notice how P prime begins to execute. It’s like watching a loop unfold in real-time. You’ll see it take steps, pause, and even reset. When it auto-plays, P prime is essentially running the logic of P while reflecting back on its own structure. This self-reference is crucial, as it shows how a program can operate based on its own description.

If you speed it up, you’ll see the looping pattern become more pronounced. Each cycle reinforces the idea that any computable function can find a fixed point that describes itself. The interplay between P and P prime beautifully illustrates how programs can reason about their own behavior, embodying the essence of the recursion theorem.
