When we talk about the complexity of chess, it’s crucial to understand that standard chess on an 8x8 board isn’t EXPTIME-complete. In fact, it’s finite and decidable, meaning you can determine the outcome in constant time, O(1), for any given position. That complexity classification primarily applies to generalized versions of chess on larger n×n boards. 

Now, don’t confuse EXPTIME-completeness with something being unsolvable in practice. Computer chess engines are incredibly advanced and can play 8x8 chess at a superhuman level. The complexity classes focus on worst-case scenarios, which doesn’t diminish the effectiveness of strategies like strong opening books or pruning techniques. 

Additionally, the 50-move rule is significant in terms of complexity but doesn’t affect actual play in a meaningful way. If you change the rules to a bounded-move variant, that shifts the complexity from EXPTIME to PSPACE. Remember, the purpose of rules like the 50-move rule in standard chess is to ensure a practical end to games, rather than to serve as a measure of complexity in theoretical terms. Understanding these nuances helps us appreciate the depth of chess beyond just the moves on the board.
