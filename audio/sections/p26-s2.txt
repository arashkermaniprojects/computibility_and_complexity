Let’s dive into the splitting rule, specifically focusing on clause width. When we deal with clauses of width one or two, we have some unique cases to consider, which often slip through the cracks. For a clause of width one, we introduce a fresh auxiliary variable, let’s call it p. This essentially allows us to pad the clause while maintaining satisfiability. The key here is that this padding forces the literal, say ℓ1, to be true in any satisfying assignment. So, in effect, we’re creating a unit clause where ℓ1 must hold.

Now, moving on to clauses of width two, we apply a similar idea, but this time we need two fresh variables, p and q. Each clause gets its own padding, and this forces a new condition: we get ℓ1 ∨ ℓ2. The logic is that if you want both clauses to be satisfied, at least one of these literals must be true. No assignment to p can satisfy both unless ℓ1 or ℓ2 already holds true. That’s how we cleverly ensure the properties we want without losing the original satisfiability of the problem.
