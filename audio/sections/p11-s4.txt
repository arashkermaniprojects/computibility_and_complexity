When discussing the CFG that generates everything except the accepting computation histories of a Turing machine, students often trip up on a few key points. First, remember that for the grammar G sub M comma w to generate everything, it means L of G sub M comma w equals Σ star only if M does not accept w. It’s easy to think backward here, so be careful not to flip that direction in your mind. 

Then there’s the reverse-write trick. This is crucial. If you don't apply it, the CFG would struggle to match the first computation step with the last in the right positions, which it simply can’t do. Instead, this trick lets you turn position matching into adjacency matching, which is vital for the CFG to function properly. 

Another common misunderstanding is about how the grammar works. It doesn’t check if the history is correct; rather, it generates incorrect histories. The valid histories are those that the grammar can’t produce, and those failures directly relate to the witnesses for A sub T M. Lastly, don’t worry about trying to make G deterministic. CFGs are inherently nondeterministic, and that’s perfectly fine. They can explore different paths for possible errors, and all we need is one valid derivation for a string.
