EQTM is undecidable · mapping reduction

From ⟨M, w⟩ produce ⟨M1, M2⟩ such that M1 ≡ M2 iff M accepts w.

CLO-2R1·30 ptsL6
1 · Theory

EQTM = {⟨M1, M2⟩ : L(M1) = L(M2)}. We construct two machines that are equal iff M accepts w.

Construction

M1: reject everything. L(M1) = ∅.

M2: on input x, ignore x; run M on w; accept iff M accepts. So L(M2) = ∅ if M doesn't accept w, else Σ*.

Then: L(M1) = L(M2) ⇔ L(M2) = ∅ ⇔ M doesn't accept w.

So ⟨M, w⟩ ∈ ATM ⇔ ⟨M1, M2⟩ ∉ EQTM. That's a mapping reduction ATM ≤m ¬EQTM.

3 · Q&A defence notes
  • Q1. Why use the empty-language M1?
    It's a fixed reference. Comparing against ∅ reduces EQTM to ETM in disguise — we already know ETM is undecidable.
  • Q2. Is EQTM recognizable?
    No. Neither EQTM nor ¬EQTM is recognizable. It sits in the "neither" region of the Venn diagram (Problem 4).
  • Q3. What's the difference between a mapping reduction and a Turing reduction?
    Mapping reduction is a single computable function that translates instances; the answer for B directly gives the answer for A. Turing reduction can call B's decider multiple times and do extra work. Mapping is stricter and preserves recognizability.
  • Q4. Why does mapping reduction preserve undecidability?
    If we could decide EQTM, composing with the reduction gives a decider for ATM — contradiction.
