Why does this work? Building C from A and B is doing real work — it is computing two DFAs in parallel, with the pair of current states treated as a single composite state. That is the standard product construction. Crucially, the class of regular languages is closed under intersection, complement, and union, so the symmetric difference — defined as A intersect not-B, union B intersect not-A — stays regular. The product construction gives you a finite-state machine for it. And that finite-state machine then plugs into the previous slide's decider. The whole trick is reducing one problem to another we have already solved.
