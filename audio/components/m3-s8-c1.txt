Computation histories are a powerful concept that allow simpler models, like linear bounded automata (LBAs) or context-free grammars (CFGs), to check the computations of Turing machines (TMs). This means that properties that are undecidable for Turing machines can also be shown to be undecidable for these simpler models. On the other hand, the recursion theorem enables any Turing machine to act as if it has its own description written on its tape. This capability is particularly useful because it helps in proving Rice's theorem, which states that any non-trivial property of the language recognized by a Turing machine is undecidable. In essence, the interplay between computation histories and the recursion theorem deepens our understanding of undecidability across various computational models. Remember, these concepts illustrate how even simple models can engage with the complexities of computation.
