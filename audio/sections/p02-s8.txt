Let’s dive into some important questions about the universal Turing machine. First, why doesn’t the universal TM decide A sub T M? Well, if a specific Turing machine M runs into an infinite loop on some input w, the universal TM will also loop forever while trying to simulate it. A decider needs to always halt, so while the universal TM can say “yes” if M accepts, it can’t definitively say “no” without the risk of running indefinitely.

Now, is the universal TM one specific machine or a family of machines? It’s actually one specific machine, designed once and for all. Its alphabet is broad enough to encode any other Turing machine's symbols and states, allowing it to simulate any machine M on input w.

How does this universal TM keep track of M’s state during the simulation? It uses a special area on its tape, where it writes down M's current state next to the position of the simulated tape head. Each time M makes a move, the universal TM checks M’s transition table from that encoded part of the tape.

If we load a scenario where M loops forever, the universal TM keeps stepping through the process, showing a status of "running..." The machine can’t tell if M will eventually halt or get stuck forever. This uncertainty is the heart of the halting problem, highlighting why deciding A sub T M is so complex.

And a bonus question: is your laptop a universal Turing machine? Morally, yes, it can simulate many things, but literally, no. A real computer has finite memory, making it essentially a very large finite automaton. The concept of universality applies in theory, but in practice, the distinction is often negligible.
