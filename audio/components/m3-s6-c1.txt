Rice's theorem says: every nontrivial semantic property of a TM's language is undecidable. Here is the proof in pictures.

Suppose, for contradiction, we have a decider R for some such property P. Because P is nontrivial, there are TMs T-yes and T-no whose languages have, and don't have, property P. Without loss of generality, pick T-no as the trivial machine that rejects everything, so L of T-no is empty.

The reduction maps angle-bracket M, w angle-bracket to angle-bracket M sub M, w angle-bracket. The TM M sub M, w on any input x first runs M on w, ignoring x. If M does not accept w, M sub M, w rejects x and stops. Otherwise, M sub M, w hands x to T-yes and copies T-yes's answer. So L of M sub M, w is L of T-yes when M accepts w, and is empty when M does not accept w.

Now feed angle-bracket M sub M, w angle-bracket to R. R tells us whether L of M sub M, w has property P. By the case analysis: yes if and only if M accepts w. We've decided A-T-M, which is impossible. So R cannot exist; property P is undecidable. This is Sipser theorem 6.10.
