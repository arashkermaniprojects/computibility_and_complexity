Let’s dive into some important questions about game complexity, specifically focusing on chess and its variations. First, why does standard 8×8 chess have an O(1) solution? The key is that a finite board has a limited number of configurations, around 10 to the 43rd or so. This means the game tree is finite. With brute-force minimax, you can analyze any starting position in constant time, as it only depends on the board’s size. And that huge constant doesn’t change the problem’s complexity class.

Next, what’s the difference between PSPACE and EXPTIME? Without a move limit, games can go on for an exponentially long time, which makes the game tree depth unbounded, pushing the problem into EXPTIME. But if you impose a polynomial move limit, you truncate the game tree to polynomial depth, bringing it back down to PSPACE.

Now, what else is EXPTIME-complete? Games like generalized Go without the ko rule, Shogi, and generalized Othello all fit this bill. These games on n×n boards represent a kind of natural ceiling for complexity. Finally, where do real games sit in the complexity hierarchy? You have easy ones like perfect-play tic-tac-toe and small-board hex at P, while bounded-move chess and TQBF games are PSPACE-complete. Full chess is classified as EXPTIME-complete. Then you have games with infinite state spaces, like Magic: The Gathering, which are practically impossible for AI to handle.
