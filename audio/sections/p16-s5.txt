Alright, let’s talk about some common pitfalls when working with self-aware Turing machines. First off, when we say “obtain ⟨M⟩,” it’s important to remember that this isn’t some sort of magic trick. The recursion theorem tells us that ⟨M⟩ is actually constructible. There’s a specific procedure to create it, like what we explored with the quine machinery in Problem 13. This isn't just hand-waving; it’s a tangible feature of the Turing machine. 

Next, be careful not to confuse M with D’s classification of M. M’s job is to produce an answer, either accept or reject for a given input w. On the other hand, D’s role is to classify whether “M accepts w” or not. The real contradiction arises when M’s answer goes against what D classifies about M. 

Another thing to keep in mind is that the proof we’re discussing generalizes well, unlike the proof for Problem 3. If you try to apply that argument to say "the language of M is empty," it quickly becomes awkward. The beauty of the recursion theorem is that it lets us build M to do the opposite of what D states about M's language, and that process flows smoothly. Lastly, remember that this theorem doesn’t allow us to bootstrap the universe; it simply states that for any computable function t, you can create a fixed point of t. It doesn’t give us the ability to compute uncomputable functions.
