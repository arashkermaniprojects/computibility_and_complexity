Let’s dive into the fascinating concept of self-aware Turing machines. Imagine a Turing machine, or TM, that can read its own description. This is made possible by something called the recursion theorem. What this theorem tells us is that any TM can effectively access a description of itself on its tape. So, when we say a TM reads its own description, it’s like it’s looking in a mirror, seeing exactly what it is made of.

Now, why is this interesting? It allows us to construct a very elegant proof that the problem A sub T M, which asks if a TM accepts a particular input, is undecidable. Here’s how it works. Assume we have a TM, let’s call it D, that can decide A sub T M. Using the recursion theorem, we can create a new TM, M. This machine takes an input, w, and first obtains its own description, ⟨M⟩. Then it calls D to check if M accepts w.

But here’s the twist: M will output the opposite of what D says. If D says M accepts w, M will reject, and vice versa. This creates a contradiction. M accepting w is equivalent to D rejecting, which means D can’t exist. So, through this clever use of self-reference, we see that A sub T M is undecidable. The diagonalization isn’t separate; it’s all packed within M itself. This is a perfect example of how powerful self-reference can be in computation!
