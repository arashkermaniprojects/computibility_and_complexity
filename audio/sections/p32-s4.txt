When we talk about self-reducibility, it's important to remember that we’re treating the decider as a black box. This means that we’re not looking inside it or trying to understand how it works. Instead, we're making calls to it, like D of phi prime. Each time we call it, we provide a residual formula that’s smaller than the original. This is crucial because it lets us keep narrowing down our problem without changing the list of variables we started with.

Now, if you think about how many times we might need to call the decider, it's at most n plus one times, where n is the number of variables. Each of these calls is working with a formula that’s still polynomial in size, which keeps our process efficient. 

This is a Turing reduction, not a many-one reduction, which is a key point. With Turing reductions, we can ask multiple queries to the oracle, and the results from one query can influence the next. This flow of information helps us find a satisfying assignment effectively. Just keep in mind that each step builds on the previous one, leveraging the power of that decider.
