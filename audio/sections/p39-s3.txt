When we talk about the pitfalls in encoding a QBF as a generalized geography game, we need to focus on a few key aspects, particularly the structure of the QBF itself. It must be in what's called alternating prenex form—this means you have a strict alternation of existential and universal quantifiers, like ∃∀∃∀. This structure mirrors the game's turn-taking. 

Now, it’s important to remember the normalization process we discussed earlier. A crucial rule is that no vertex can be revisited. This is where player 1 can get trapped. If player 1 chooses a literal that turns out to be false, they can’t go back to the variable that was already visited because of that no-revisit rule. Essentially, the literal they picked connects to a target that they’ve already been to. 

While the process of building this game graph is polynomial time, playing the game optimally is another story. That’s where things get tough—it remains PSPACE-hard. So, understanding these pitfalls is essential for navigating the complexities of the game and the QBF structure.
