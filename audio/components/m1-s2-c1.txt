The proof that A sub DFA is decidable is direct, almost trivial — and that is the point. On input angle-B, w, the decider simulates B on w by tracking the current state. Start at q-zero. Read the first symbol of w, apply the transition function to get the next state. Read the next symbol, apply the transition again. Keep going until you reach the end of w. Then check whether the final state is in B's accept set. Accept if yes, reject if no. The simulation takes time proportional to the length of w, and it always halts because w has finite length. That is your decider, and that is what makes A sub DFA decidable.
