Alright, let’s talk about some common pitfalls that students often encounter when working with this reduction from the A sub T M problem to the complement of E sub T M. One big mistake is thinking that M prime runs M on the input w. That’s not right! M prime actually runs M on a fixed w that’s hard-coded inside it. If you do it the other way around, the whole trick falls apart.

Another thing to keep in mind is the direction of the reduction. We need to show A sub T M is reducible to E sub T M, not the opposite. Remember, the problem we know is undecidable has to be the one feeding into the one we’re trying to prove undecidable. 

Students also get mixed up between E sub T M and its complement, ¬E sub T M. The reduction shows that M accepts w if and only if the language of M prime is all possible strings, which tells us that the encoding of M prime doesn’t belong to E sub T M. 

Finally, some think M prime has to halt to be valid. That’s not the case! A Turing machine can be valid even if it loops forever on every input, and a language that’s empty is just fine. The key is to correctly set L of M prime to be all strings versus empty, because that’s what E sub T M is asking about.
