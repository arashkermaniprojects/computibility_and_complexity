When working with the implication-graph trick, it’s crucial to remember that it really only applies to 2-clauses. Once you step into k-clauses where k is 3 or greater, things get a lot more complicated. With 3-clauses, you have multiple ways that implications can combine, and that creates a non-local structure that the implication graph just can't handle effectively. This is one reason why 2-SAT sits comfortably in P, while 3-SAT is NP-complete. 

Now, it’s also interesting to note how 2-SAT fits snugly within nondeterministic logarithmic space, or NL. You can prove that UNSAT is also in NL, thanks to the Immerman–Szelepcsényi theorem. Both 2-SAT and its negation, ¬2-SAT, are in NL. This leads to a neat fact: for 2-SAT, the decision problem and the search problem are effectively the same. Both can be solved efficiently, not just giving you a yes or no answer, but actually providing a satisfying assignment. So, while 2-SAT is manageable and fits within these nice frameworks, once we go to 3-SAT, we really start seeing the complexity kick in.
