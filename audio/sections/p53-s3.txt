When you're working with empirical data to analyze the running time of a Turing machine, it’s crucial to remember that the data can suggest trends, but it doesn’t prove anything definitively. For instance, if you find a nice quadratic curve through your data points, it might seem like you’ve confirmed that the complexity is O(n²). But be careful! There could be hidden factors, like a log term or a constant, that aren’t visible when you’re only looking at small input sizes.

To get a better picture, it’s helpful to calculate steps divided by n squared. If that ratio approaches a constant as n increases, then you can confidently say you have Θ(n²). However, if that ratio keeps increasing, then the actual complexity is higher than quadratic. 

Also, stay vigilant for outliers. The same Turing machine can behave very differently on different inputs of the same length. It’s essential to focus on worst-case scenarios instead of average cases, as worst-case profiling gives you a more accurate understanding of the machine's performance.
