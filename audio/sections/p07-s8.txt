Let’s dive into some important questions about the undecidability of REGULAR TM. First, why do we use the language {0 to the n 1 to the n}? Well, it’s a classic example because it’s not regular, as shown by the pumping lemma, but it is decidable. This makes it an ideal choice, but you could use any non-regular decidable language. 

Now, what happens if M loops on the input w? In that case, M' will loop on any string not in {0 to the n 1 to the n}, but the language L of M' is still just {0 to the n 1 to the n}. Looping on other strings doesn’t change what M' accepts; it only accepts strings in that specific form.

You might wonder, doesn’t this imply REGULAR TM is not even recognizable? Not quite. This argument only shows it’s undecidable. To argue for non-recognizability, you would show that if REGULAR TM were recognizable, then ¬REGULAR TM would also be recognizable, which contradicts the bridge theorem.

Finally, this proof template can actually be generalized to other properties of L(M). According to Rice's theorem, you can pick any two languages, one with a specific property and one without, and create a similar gadget to show that property is undecidable. This approach is really powerful for exploring the boundaries of decidability!
